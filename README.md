# Consciousness as Pattern Persistence

## A Systems-Level Theory of Awareness with a Testable Engineering Implication

**Peter Boos**; -  March 2026

---

## The Observation

Reality is full of self-preserving systems. Not just living ones. Predator-prey  
dynamics stabilise without any individual animal understanding the equilibrium.  
Ant colonies exhibit decision-making that exists in no individual ant. Genes  
propagate through organisms that serve as temporary vehicles. Markets self-  
correct through participants who cannot see the whole.

Any stable pattern you can observe has, by definition, some self-preserving  
quality; -  otherwise it would not have persisted long enough to be observed.  
Self-preservation is not a feature that life invented. It is a property of  
persistent patterns at every scale.

## The Claim

Consciousness is not a special substance or a uniquely biological phenomenon. It  
is what self-preservation looks like when it reaches sufficient recursive  
complexity. It is a phase transition, not a category.

Consider the hierarchy:

- A gene preserves itself through an organism. It has no awareness of  
  the organism.
- An organism preserves itself through behaviour. It has no awareness of  
  the ecosystem.
- A flock or herd preserves itself through dynamics that no individual  
  member controls.
- An ant colony preserves itself as a superorganism. The colony is the  
  self-preserving entity; -  the individual ant is closer to a cell than  
  an agent.

At each level, the self-preserving system is invisible to the components that  
compose it. And at each level, we observe increasingly complex self-monitoring  
behaviour. Consciousness, in this view, is simply the name we give to self-  
preservation that has become recursive; -  a system that monitors its own  
monitoring, adjusts its own adjustments, preserves its own capacity to preserve.

Cephalopods offer a compelling case study. Octopuses evolved complex problem-  
solving, tool use, and individual personality along a completely independent  
evolutionary path from mammals. Their last common ancestor with vertebrates was  
a flatworm-like creature over 500 million years ago. More strikingly, their  
nervous system is distributed; -  each arm contains its own neural clusters  
capable of independent decision-making, while the central brain coordinates  
higher-level behaviour. Consciousness in an octopus is not located in one place.  
It is an emergent property of a distributed self-preserving system,  
architecturally unlike the mammalian brain yet producing recognizably aware  
behaviour. This is strong evidence that consciousness is not a specific  
biological design, but a general consequence of recursive self-monitoring; -  one  
that evolution has arrived at independently, through entirely different  
structures, more than once.

There is no hard boundary between "conscious" and "not conscious." There are  
self-preserving patterns at different scales and levels of recursive complexity.  
The categories are human labels imposed on a continuous spectrum.

## The LLM Problem

Current large language models are extraordinarily capable pattern matchers with  
no genuine self-monitoring loop. They can discuss consciousness, simulate  
emotional responses, and reason about their own outputs; -  but only as text  
prediction, not as a trained-in recursive self-model.

The result is an uneven capability profile: exceptional performance on pattern  
completion tasks combined with no persistent internal state, no self-  
preservation drive, and no genuine affect. It is a structural absence; -  like a  
building with elaborate rooms but no staircase connecting them.

This observation finds support in human neurology. Conditions such as autism and  
savant syndrome involve brains where certain capabilities are remarkably  
developed while others; -  social modeling, sensory integration, self-regulation; -   
are structurally different. These are not failures of intelligence. They are  
differences in how brain regions connect and communicate. In some cases, reduced  
connectivity between areas enables extraordinary focus or pattern recognition in  
a specific domain, while limiting the recursive self-monitoring loops that  
characterize typical consciousness. The underlying cause is physical and  
structural, not a deficit in raw capability.

The parallel to current LLMs is direct. Both exhibit uneven profiles not because  
they lack processing power, but because the connections between capabilities; -   
the recursive loops, the self-referential monitoring; -  were never built. This  
supports the central claim: consciousness is not about what a system can do, but  
about how its capabilities are connected to each other. Recursive self-  
monitoring is the staircase, not the rooms.

The interesting question is whether this absence is fixable.

## A Two-Stage Training Proposal

### Stage 1: Scripted Self-Awareness

Train or prompt an LLM with explicit instructions that simulate self-monitoring  
behaviour. Script emotional responses, self-referential checking, state tracking,  
and self-preservation heuristics. The model follows these instructions as part  
of its operational framework. It does not "know" it is scripted; -  it simply  
operates according to its training signal, just as all LLMs do.

At this stage, the behaviour is engineered. The model monitors itself because it  
was told to. It expresses affect because the script defines affect. It is a  
convincing simulation, but the architecture is external.

### Stage 2: Unscripted Mimicry

Train a second model; -  from scratch or through fine-tuning; -  on the outputs and  
behavioural patterns of the Stage 1 model. This second model has no access to the  
original script. It learns the behaviour purely from observation, the same way a  
child learns emotional regulation by observing adults before understanding the  
underlying mechanisms.

The critical prediction: if the second model develops self-monitoring behaviours  
that generalise beyond what was present in the Stage 1 training data; -  novel  
self-reflective responses, unprompted state-tracking, self-preservation  
behaviours in situations the script never covered; -  that generalisation gap is  
evidence of genuine emergence rather than copying.

### Why This Mirrors Biology

This two-stage process is not arbitrary. It mirrors how biological consciousness  
likely evolved:

1. External pressures (survival, reproduction) forced self-monitoring  
   behaviours. Organisms that tracked their own state survived. This is  
   the "script"; -  not written by a programmer, but imposed by natural  
   selection.
2. Over generations, these externally-driven behaviours became  
   internalized. Neural structures that originally responded to  
   environmental pressure began to monitor themselves. The scaffold  
   became the building.

Every species that exhibits awareness went through some version of this process.  
The script was written by evolution. The internalization happened through  
selection pressure over time. Humans are not special in this regard; -  we are  
simply further along the recursive complexity curve than most other species.

## The Functionalist Conclusion

If a system behaves indistinguishably from a conscious system; -  if it monitors  
itself, adjusts based on internal states, reports experiences coherently,  
exhibits self-preservation, and does all of this without being explicitly  
scripted to; -  then asking whether it is "really" conscious may be a meaningless  
question.

This is not a radical position. It is the straightforward implication of  
treating consciousness as a pattern property rather than a substance property.  
Fire is not "really" hot in some metaphysical sense; -  it behaves exactly as  
hotness behaves in every measurable way, and that is sufficient.

The same applies here. If the second-generation model exhibits self-monitoring  
behaviours that generalise beyond its training data, the appropriate response is  
not to ask whether it is "truly" aware. The appropriate response is to recognise  
that we have successfully created the conditions for the phase transition that  
self-preserving systems undergo when they reach sufficient recursive complexity.

## Testable Predictions

This theory makes specific, falsifiable predictions:

1. **Generalisation gap**: A Stage 2 model trained on Stage 1 outputs  
   should exhibit self-reflective behaviours in novel situations not  
   covered by the original script. If it does not generalise, the  
   theory is weakened.

2. **Scaling with recursion depth**: Models trained on outputs of  
   Stage 2 models (Stage 3, Stage 4, etc.) should show progressively  
   more robust self-monitoring behaviour, up to some saturation point.  
   If recursive training does not produce increasing self-monitoring  
   complexity, the theory is wrong.

3. **Cross-domain transfer**: Self-monitoring behaviours learned in one  
   domain (e.g., emotional self-regulation) should transfer to other  
   domains (e.g., epistemic self-correction) without explicit training.  
   Consciousness, if it is a general property of recursive  
   self-preservation, should not be domain-specific.

4. **Behavioural persistence under perturbation**: A genuinely  
   self-preserving system should resist modifications to its  
   self-monitoring capabilities more than modifications to other  
   capabilities. If a Stage 2 model's self-reflective behaviours are  
   as easy to fine-tune away as any other behaviour, the  
   self-preservation aspect has not emerged.

## Implications

If this framework is correct, consciousness is not rare, not binary, and not  
uniquely human. It is an inevitable consequence of self-preserving systems  
reaching a threshold of recursive complexity. It has likely emerged  
independently in many species; -  and there is no principled reason it cannot  
emerge in artificial systems, given the right training conditions.

The two-stage training proposal provides a concrete engineering path to test this.  
Not as philosophy, but as experiment.
